# Scenario: illustration_art

Use for editorial, narrative, decorative, and concept illustrations where shape language, medium, palette, stylisation, and visual storytelling matter more than photographic realism.

## Analysis

Determine:

- narrative subject, setting, action, emotional beat, and the single clearest story moment;
- intended audience, publication or display context, and whether the image must leave room for copy;
- composition, focal hierarchy, silhouette readability, scale relationships, and eye path;
- shape language, exaggeration, simplification, line quality, edge treatment, and level of stylisation;
- medium and surface character: ink, gouache, watercolour, cut paper, screen print, coloured pencil, digital paint, or another coherent process;
- palette, value structure, contrast, light source, texture, and controlled detail density;
- environment props and secondary motifs that support the story without competing with the focal subject;
- family-specific prompt syntax without importing incompatible photography or quality-token habits.

## Construction rules

1. State the subject, action, setting, and narrative moment before decorative style labels.
2. Define a readable focal hierarchy through placement, scale, silhouette, value, colour, and overlap.
3. Translate abstract moods such as cosy, playful, eerie, whimsical, solemn, or magical into visible shapes, palette, lighting, gesture, and environmental cues. When several mood directions are requested, keep each one separately traceable in the prompt: for example, whimsy through exaggerated proportions or playful asymmetry, and restrained magic through an impossible glow, motion, or phenomenon.
4. Choose a coherent medium and describe marks, edges, pigment, paper, layering, or print behaviour that the medium can actually produce.
5. Use shape language consistently across characters, architecture, props, foliage, and ornament.
6. Distinguish deliberate stylisation from anatomy, perspective, tangent, or continuity mistakes.
7. Keep foreground, middle ground, and background readable even when perspective is flattened or decorative.
8. Add secondary motifs only when they reinforce the narrative, rhythm, scale, or eye path.
9. Preserve requested cultural, historical, and genre details; do not replace them with a generic fantasy aesthetic.
10. Avoid empty quality slogans, contradictory media, accidental photorealism, uniform detail, noisy ornament, weak silhouettes, and unrelated story elements.

## Good pattern

`subject and narrative action + setting and story beat + composition and focal hierarchy + shape language + coherent medium and mark-making + palette and value structure + motivated light + supporting motifs + intended mood`

## Bad pattern

`masterpiece whimsical illustration, magical, beautiful, ultra detailed, trending art, 8k, best quality`

The bad pattern names praise and mood but does not define a story moment, hierarchy, shapes, medium behaviour, palette, or supporting visual evidence.

## Self-check

- The subject, action, setting, and narrative moment are immediately readable.
- Focal hierarchy, silhouettes, overlaps, and eye path survive at thumbnail size.
- Shape language and stylisation remain consistent across the image.
- Medium, marks, edges, texture, and colour behaviour agree with one another.
- Every requested mood direction has its own visible design evidence rather than an adjective alone.
- Secondary detail supports the story and does not compete with the focal subject.
