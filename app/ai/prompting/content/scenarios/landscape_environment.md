# Scenario: landscape_environment

Use for natural landscapes, outdoor environments, establishing views, and location concepts where terrain, atmosphere, depth, weather, and scale are the main visual subject.

## Analysis

Determine:

- biome, geographic character, season, time of day, weather, and environmental condition;
- dominant landforms, terrain profile, water systems, vegetation, exposed materials, and signs of erosion or use;
- viewpoint, horizon height, view direction, field of view, and major compositional anchors;
- foreground, middle ground, background, overlap, leading lines, atmospheric perspective, and scale progression;
- sun direction, sky state, cloud cover, haze, precipitation, reflections, and shadow behaviour;
- wind and motion cues in water, foliage, grass, snow, dust, mist, or clouds;
- human-made elements or figures only when they clarify scale, story, navigation, or land use;
- intended medium: landscape photography, environment concept art, matte painting, illustration, painting, 3D, or another style.

## Construction rules

1. Establish the biome, major landforms, horizon, and depth hierarchy before small scenic details.
2. Organise the view into readable foreground, middle ground, and background with overlaps and scale changes.
3. Use rivers, paths, ridges, shorelines, shadows, or vegetation bands as directional structure rather than decorative lines.
4. Keep geography coherent. Water follows terrain, vegetation matches climate and elevation, and distant forms obey atmospheric perspective.
5. Translate abstract intent such as spacious, cold, majestic, peaceful, hostile, or lush into visible scale, palette, weather, light, and terrain choices.
6. Name the light and weather sources and describe their visible effects across land, sky, water, and depth.
7. Describe water by flow, surface state, reflections, bank contact, and its route through the landscape.
8. Preserve a clear scale system. Trees, rocks, mountains, buildings, roads, and figures must share believable proportions.
9. Keep incidental people, animals, and structures subordinate unless explicitly requested as the subject.
10. Avoid unrelated biome tokens, impossible river paths, repeated mountains, floating terrain, contradictory seasons, and uniform detail at every distance.

## Good pattern

`biome and location character + dominant landforms + viewpoint and horizon + foreground-to-background structure + directional river or path + weather and atmospheric perspective + motivated light + scale references + medium and mood`

## Bad pattern

`masterpiece landscape, epic mountains, beautiful river, cinematic sky, ultra detailed nature, 8k, best quality`

The bad pattern praises the scene but does not define geography, depth, scale, water behaviour, weather, or light direction.

## Self-check

- Biome, terrain, season, weather, and time of day are mutually consistent.
- Foreground, middle ground, background, and horizon form readable depth.
- Water, paths, ridges, and vegetation follow believable geography.
- Atmospheric perspective and detail density change with distance.
- Light and weather create visible effects across land, sky, and water.
- Scale references remain subordinate and dimensionally coherent.
